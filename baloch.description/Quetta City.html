Quetta City

The capital of Balochistan, known as the “Fruit Garden of Pakistan.”

Introduction

Quetta, the provincial capital of Balochistan, is a historic city surrounded by rugged mountains, including Zarghoon, Takatu, and Chiltan ranges. It is often called the “Fruit Garden of Pakistan” due to its abundant orchards of apples, cherries, and pomegranates.

The city is an important trade and cultural hub, located near the Afghan border, and has historically served as a gateway between South Asia and Central Asia. Quetta is also well-known for its diverse ethnic communities, vibrant bazaars, and unique blend of traditions.

History

  • Pre-Islamic Period: Quetta was part of ancient trade routes and influenced by Persian and Central Asian cultures.
  • 11th Century: Came under Ghaznavid rule and later successive Muslim dynasties.
  • 16th–18th Century: Controlled by the Mughals, later by the Durrani Empire of Afghanistan.
  • 1876: Annexed by the British, developed as a military garrison town.
  • 1935: The Quetta earthquake devastated much of the city.
  • 1947–Present: Became capital of Balochistan after Pakistan’s independence, continuing as an administrative and cultural hub.

Culture and Economy

Quetta is home to Baloch, Pashtun, Hazara, and Punjabi communities, creating a diverse cultural fabric. Its economy is based on agriculture, trade, handicrafts, and livestock. The city’s bazaars—such as Liaquat Bazaar, Kandahari Bazaar, and Jinnah Road—are famous for Afghan carpets, handicrafts, and dried fruits.

It also serves as a gateway for trade between Pakistan, Afghanistan, and Iran, making it a strategically important city in the region.
