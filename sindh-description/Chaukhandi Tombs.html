Chaukhandi Tombs

A historic Islamic necropolis famous for its unique sandstone carvings, located near Karachi, Sindh.

Introduction

The Chaukhandi Tombs, situated about 29 km east of Karachi along the National Highway, form a remarkable early-Islamic cemetery in Sindh. Dating from the 15th to 18th centuries, these tombs are primarily associated with the Jokhio tribe and other Sindhi-Baloch clans.

Recognized for their intricate sandstone carvings and unique architectural style, the tombs are included on UNESCO’s Tentative World Heritage list. They remain one of Pakistan’s most important heritage sites.

History

  • 15th–18th centuries: Tombs built during Mughal-era Sindh for Jokhio and allied tribes.
  • 19th century: Many tombs fell into decline due to neglect and weather erosion.
  • 1993: Added to UNESCO’s Tentative World Heritage List.
  • 21st century: Threatened by encroachment, theft, and environmental degradation.
  • 2023: Sindh government proposed creating an archaeological park for preservation.

Architecture

The tombs are built from yellow sandstone, typically arranged in pyramid-like structures. Each tomb is formed by stacked slabs decorated with floral, geometric, and figurative motifs. Male graves often show carvings of weapons and horsemen, while female tombs display jewelry patterns such as necklaces and bangles.

Some tombs are sheltered by pillared canopies resembling Hindu pavilions. Unlike most Islamic cemeteries, these graves are oriented south-to-north, a unique feature of the Chaukhandi style.

Visitor Information

The site is easily accessible from Karachi via the N-5 highway. Entry is free, though local guides often provide insights for small tips. Best visited in cooler months (November–March). The area has limited facilities, so travelers should plan accordingly.
