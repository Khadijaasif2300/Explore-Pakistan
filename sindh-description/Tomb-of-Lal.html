Tomb of Lal Shahbaz Qalandar

A majestic Sufi mausoleum and vibrant place of devotion in Sehwan Sharif, Sindh.

Introduction

The Tomb of Lal Shahbaz Qalandar is a revered Sufi shrine in Sehwan Sharif, Sindh, honoring the 13th-century mystic Lal Shahbaz Qalandar (1177–1274). His teachings emphasize spiritual love, tolerance, and unity among diverse communities. The shrine draws tens of thousands of pilgrims annually. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

The complex is an architectural gem, featuring glazed tilework (Sindhi kashi-tiles), mirror mosaics, marble panels, and a gold-plated entrance that reflects its spiritual and cultural significance. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

History

  • 1356 CE: Original shrine constructed during the reign of Feroz Shah Tughlaq. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}
  • 1639: Expanded by the Tarkhan rulers—courtyard paved with glazed tiles by Mirza Dindar Khan. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}
  • 20th century renovations: Mir Karam Ali Talpur added silver embellishments; later Prime Minister Zulfikar Ali Bhutto installed the gold-plated door. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}
  • 1994 & beyond: Post-dome collapse renovation, including a gilded dome and enlarged dhamaal courtyard. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

Architecture & Features

The shrine features a majestic gilded dome (approximately 110 ft high and 56 ft diameter) with mirrored interiors and glazed tiles, creating a luminous, spiritually uplifting space. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

The inner sanctum (approx. 100 sq yards) houses the silver-canopied tomb. The perimeter includes Quran-standing racks and rows of lit oil lamps, enhancing the shrine's atmosphere of devotion and reflection. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

Annual Urs Festival

The Urs, held on 18 Sha'ban of the Islamic calendar, attracts over half a million pilgrims in celebration of spiritual union through music, dance (dhamaal), and devotion. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}

In February 2017, the shrine was attacked in a tragic suicide bombing that took 88 lives. Despite this, the ritual dhamaal resumed the very next evening—symbolizing resilience and faith. :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}
