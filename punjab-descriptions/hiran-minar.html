Hiran Minar

A unique Mughal-era monument in Sheikhupura, built as a hunting retreat and memorial.

Introduction

Hiran Minar, located in Sheikhupura, Punjab, Pakistan, is a 17th-century Mughal complex built during the reign of Emperor Jahangir. The site was constructed in memory of his beloved pet antelope, Mansraj, symbolizing the Mughal affinity for nature and animals. Surrounded by a large water tank and connected with causeways, Hiran Minar served as both a hunting retreat and a monument of remembrance.

History

  • 1606: Emperor Jahangir ordered the construction of Hiran Minar in memory of his pet antelope, Mansraj.
  • Early 17th Century: The site developed as a royal hunting ground and leisure resort for the Mughal court.
  • Mughal Era: Used as a retreat where the emperor and nobles engaged in hunting expeditions.
  • Present: Preserved as a heritage site, attracting historians, tourists, and nature enthusiasts.

Architecture

The main feature of Hiran Minar is a tall minaret, approximately 100 feet high, built in memory of the antelope. The structure is surrounded by a vast rectangular water tank measuring about 750 by 895 feet, with brick walls and stone steps leading down to the water.

At the center of the tank stands a square pavilion, accessible through four stone causeways aligned with the cardinal directions. The blend of the monument with its natural surroundings reflects Mughal aesthetics of harmony between architecture and landscape.
