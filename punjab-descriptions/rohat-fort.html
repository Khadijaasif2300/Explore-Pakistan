Rohtas Fort

A UNESCO World Heritage Site near Jhelum, Pakistan.

Introduction

Rohtas Fort, located near Jhelum in Punjab, Pakistan, is a massive 16th-century fortress built by Sher Shah Suri between 1541 and 1548. The fort was constructed to serve as a stronghold against the Mughal emperor Humayun and to suppress the local Gakhar tribes of the region. It is one of the largest and most formidable forts in South Asia, known for its exceptional defensive architecture that combines Afghan and Persian styles.

History

  • 1541: Construction of Rohtas Fort began under Sher Shah Suri.
  • 1548: Completion of the fort after seven years of construction.
  • Mughal Era: The fort came under Mughal control but was rarely used as a royal residence.
  • Sikh Period: Used as a garrison by the Sikh rulers.
  • British Era: The British used the fort for military purposes.
  • 1997: Rohtas Fort was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site for its exceptional military architecture.

Architecture

Rohtas Fort covers an area of about 70 hectares and is enclosed by massive stone walls stretching over 4 km, varying in thickness between 10 and 18 feet. The walls are reinforced by 68 bastions and pierced with 12 imposing gates.

The gates, such as Sohail Gate, Kabuli Gate, and Shishi Gate, are the most prominent features of the fort. They are decorated with stone carvings, inscriptions, and glazed tiles.

The fort has several internal structures including stepwells (baolis) for water, mosques, and residential quarters for soldiers. The Haveli of Man Singh is the only surviving palace structure inside the fort.
