Shah Rukn-e-Alam’s Tomb

An iconic Sufi shrine in Multan, symbolizing devotion, spirituality, and stunning architecture.

Introduction

The Tomb of Shah Rukn-e-Alam is one of the most important and magnificent monuments of Multan, Punjab, Pakistan. It is the mausoleum of the revered Sufi saint Sheikh Rukn-ud-Din Abul Fateh (commonly known as Shah Rukn-e-Alam), who lived during the 13th–14th century. Built between 1320 and 1324 CE, the tomb stands as a masterpiece of pre-Mughal architecture, blending spiritual significance with architectural grandeur.

History

  • 1320–1324 CE: The tomb was commissioned by Ghiyas-ud-Din Tughlaq, originally intended as his own mausoleum, but later dedicated to Shah Rukn-e-Alam.
  • 1330 CE: After Shah Rukn-e-Alam’s death, he was buried here, making the site a center of Sufi devotion.
  • Medieval Era: The shrine became a focal point of Islamic mysticism and spiritual gatherings in South Asia.
  • Modern Times: It remains one of Pakistan’s most visited Sufi shrines, attracting pilgrims and tourists alike.

Architecture

The mausoleum is an octagonal structure built of red brick and decorated with beautiful blue and white glazed tiles, a hallmark of Multan’s craftsmanship. Rising to a height of 35 meters, the tomb is topped by a massive white dome that dominates the skyline of Multan.

Its architectural design is considered a forerunner of Mughal architecture, featuring tapering walls, decorative battlements, arched niches, and wooden beams. The use of colored tiles and geometric motifs reflects early Indo-Islamic art and the spiritual essence of Sufi shrines.
