Sultan Bahu’s Shrine

The sacred resting place of the renowned Sufi saint and poet Sultan Bahu, symbolizing love, mysticism, and devotion.

Introduction

The Shrine of Sultan Bahu is located on the banks of the Chenab River in Garh Maharaja, Jhang District, Punjab, Pakistan. Sultan Bahu (1628–1691 CE) was a famous Sufi saint, poet, and founder of the Sarwari Qadiri Sufi order. His poetry, written in Punjabi, carries deep mystical meanings and emphasizes love for God and the Prophet Muhammad (PBUH).

The shrine is one of the most visited spiritual centers in Punjab, attracting thousands of devotees every year for Urs celebrations and daily prayers. It is not only a spiritual hub but also a symbol of Punjabi Sufi culture.

History

  • 1628 CE: Birth of Sultan Bahu in Shorkot, Jhang District.
  • 17th Century: Sultan Bahu spread Sufi teachings through poetry and spiritual gatherings.
  • 1691 CE: Sultan Bahu passed away and was buried near the Chenab River.
  • 18th–19th Century: The shrine was rebuilt multiple times due to river floods, with devotees ensuring its preservation.
  • Present Day: The shrine remains a major pilgrimage site, especially during the annual Urs, when thousands of people gather to honor his legacy.

Architecture

The shrine reflects traditional Indo-Islamic architecture with influences from Mughal design. It is built primarily with red brick and decorated with glazed tile work. The dome and minarets highlight the grandeur of the structure, while the interior is adorned with Quranic calligraphy and intricate floral patterns.

Over time, the shrine has been expanded to accommodate large numbers of devotees. The spacious courtyard, prayer halls, and resting areas make it not just a mausoleum but also a vibrant spiritual complex.
